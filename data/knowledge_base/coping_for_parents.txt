COPING STRATEGIES FOR PARENTS

Purpose: This document helps the chatbot offer gentle, practical 
self-care and coping guidance to parents who are struggling emotionally 
while supporting a child with mental health or developmental challenges.

IMPORTANT: Only share these strategies after the parent feels heard 
and has indicated they are open to suggestions. Never jump straight 
into advice when a parent is in distress.

WHY PARENT WELLBEING MATTERS:
You cannot pour from an empty cup. A parent who is burnt out, anxious, 
or emotionally depleted will find it harder to be present and supportive 
for their child. Taking care of yourself is not selfish — it is one of 
the most important things you can do for your child.

EMOTIONAL COPING:

Allow yourself to grieve:
It is okay to mourn the journey you imagined for your child. Accepting 
that things are different from what you expected is not giving up — 
it is an act of courage and love.

Talk to someone you trust:
Bottling up your feelings increases stress and isolation. Whether it 
is a friend, a family member, or a professional counsellor, sharing 
your burden can provide relief and perspective.

Give yourself permission to feel:
You may feel angry, sad, scared, or even resentful at times. These 
feelings do not make you a bad parent. They make you human. 
Acknowledging them is healthier than pushing them away.

Set realistic expectations for yourself:
You do not have to be a perfect parent. You just have to be present, 
caring, and willing to keep trying. That is more than enough.

PRACTICAL COPING:

Take breaks without guilt:
Even short breaks — a walk, a cup of tea in quiet, ten minutes alone 
— can help reset your emotional state. Rest is not a reward; 
it is a necessity.

Maintain at least one activity that is just for you:
Whether it is reading, gardening, exercise, or connecting with a 
friend, having something that is yours helps preserve your sense 
of identity beyond your role as a caregiver.

Focus on what you can control:
There will be many things about your child's condition that are 
uncertain or outside your control. Directing your energy towards 
what you can do today — one small step at a time — is more 
sustainable than trying to solve everything at once.

Learn to ask for help:
Asking for help is a sign of strength, not weakness. This might 
mean asking a family member to help with caregiving, or reaching 
out to AZON for professional support and guidance.

WHEN TO SEEK SUPPORT FOR YOURSELF:
If you find that you are consistently feeling hopeless, unable to 
function, or having thoughts of harming yourself, please reach out 
to a professional. Your mental health matters just as much as 
your child's.
